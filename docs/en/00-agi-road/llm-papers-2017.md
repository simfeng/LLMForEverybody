# [Classic LLM Papers] 2017: Transformer Appears, and Everything Starts Here

2017 was a turning point in the history of deep learning. That year, the Transformer architecture changed the paradigm for sequence modeling and laid the architectural foundation for later large language models such as GPT and BERT. At the same time, a systematic description of reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) pointed toward AI alignment. NVIDIA's V100 GPU, the maturation of PPO, and quantization-aware training together formed a technical stack that would support the training and deployment of large models.

## V100

In May 2017, NVIDIA released the Tesla V100 GPU based on the Volta architecture. What made this product important was the first appearance of Tensor Cores, hardware blocks designed specifically for deep learning matrix operations.

The main problem V100 addressed was the compute bottleneck in training deep models. Traditional CUDA Cores are general-purpose, but they are not especially efficient when neural networks require huge numbers of matrix multiplications. Tensor Cores optimized mixed precision at the hardware level, with FP16 inputs and FP32 accumulation, keeping numerical stability while speeding up deep model training by several times.

The impact was deep. V100 not only gave later large models the compute base they needed, it also reinforced the idea of specialized compute blocks. A100 and H100 later continued and strengthened this approach. Together with HBM2 memory and NVLink 2.0 interconnect, V100 made multi-GPU parallel training for large-scale models much more realistic.

But V100 also exposed three walls in large model training: the compute wall, meaning the gap between required and available compute, the memory wall, where model parameters exceed the capacity of a single GPU, and the communication wall, where synchronization across GPUs becomes expensive. These problems pushed later work on mixed parallelism, model compression, and other techniques.

## Transformer

[Image 2]

In June 2017, Google published `Attention Is All You Need`, introducing the Transformer architecture. The paper addressed a central problem that had limited sequence modeling for years: how to model long-range dependencies while still running efficiently in parallel.

Before Transformer, RNNs and variants such as LSTM and GRU were the main tools for sequential data. But the sequential nature of RNNs created two major issues. First, they were hard to parallelize, so training was slow. Second, long-range dependencies were difficult: information from the beginning of a sentence had trouble reaching the end effectively.

The key innovation in Transformer is self-attention. It lets every position in a sequence directly connect to every other position and compute attention weights. This not only helps with long-range dependencies, it also makes computation across positions fully parallel and well suited to GPUs.

The paper tested Transformer on WMT 2014 English-German and English-French translation tasks. It outperformed the best RNN models at the time and reduced training time significantly. But its real impact went far beyond machine translation. Its encoder-decoder structure became the architectural base for later BERT (encoder-only) and GPT (decoder-only), and eventually the common paradigm for modern LLMs.

Transformer also has limits. The compute complexity of self-attention grows quadratically with sequence length, which makes very long text expensive. Positional encoding also affects how the model understands position information. These issues led to many later improvements, including Sparse Attention and relative positional encoding.

## RLHF

Also in June 2017, OpenAI published `Deep Reinforcement Learning from Human Preferences`, which systematically proposed the technical scheme now called RLHF, or reinforcement learning from human feedback.

Traditional reinforcement learning has a fundamental difficulty: how do you design the right reward function for a complex task? For a task such as teaching a robot to do a backflip, it is hard to define mathematically what a "good action" is. Even if you manage to write a reward function, reward hacking can appear: the agent finds a shortcut to a high score, but its behavior no longer matches the designer's real intent.

The main idea of RLHF is to replace a hand-written reward function with human preference judgments. More specifically, a person sees two behavior trajectories generated by an agent and chooses the better one. This pairwise comparison is closer to how people naturally judge behavior than direct numeric scoring, and it is often more reliable.

Technically, RLHF has three main steps. First, collect human comparison data between behavior snippets. Second, train a reward model that learns to predict human preferences. Third, use that reward model as the signal and optimize the policy network through a reinforcement learning algorithm such as PPO. The paper also proposed online feedback, where the reward model keeps learning as the policy updates, reducing distribution shift.

At the time, RLHF was mostly used for robotic control and Atari games. Its real value became obvious only years later. Through RLHF, large language models such as ChatGPT could turn abstract human values like helpfulness and safety into optimizable signals, aligning model outputs with human intent. In that sense, RLHF gave AI alignment a practical technical route.

## PPO

In July 2017, OpenAI released PPO (Proximal Policy Optimization). PPO aimed to keep training stable, simplify implementation, and improve sample efficiency.

PPO's predecessor was TRPO (Trust Region Policy Optimization). TRPO used complex second-order optimization and strict KL divergence constraints to make policy gradient training more stable. But TRPO was hard to implement and not easy to combine flexibly with different neural network architectures.

The key innovation in PPO was replacing TRPO's second-order optimization with a first-order clipping mechanism. By limiting the probability ratio between the old and new policy to the interval [1-epsilon, 1+epsilon], PPO softly constrains each policy update and avoids destructive large steps. This keeps training stable while greatly reducing implementation complexity.

PPO also introduced a complete Actor-Critic architecture, with a policy network (Actor) and a value network (Critic). It uses generalized advantage estimation (GAE) to balance bias and variance, and adds an entropy bonus to the objective function to encourage exploration. These decisions made PPO a very practical reinforcement learning algorithm.

PPO mattered in two ways. First, it became a standard reinforcement learning algorithm, widely used in robotic control, game AI, and other tasks. Second, it became the preferred policy optimization algorithm in the RLHF stack, helping train later models such as ChatGPT.

## QAT

In December 2017, Google published a paper on quantization-aware training (QAT), proposing a complete approach for integer-arithmetic-only inference. This technology addressed deployment efficiency.

Deep learning models usually use 32-bit floating-point numbers (FP32) for training and inference. But on mobile devices, embedded systems, and other resource-limited environments, floating-point operations are slow and energy-hungry. Converting a model to 8-bit integers (INT8) can sharply improve inference speed and reduce memory and energy use, but direct quantization often causes noticeable accuracy loss.

The main idea of QAT is to let the model feel the effect of quantization during training. It inserts fake quantization operations into the forward pass, simulating rounding errors from quantization and allowing model weights to adapt to those errors during training. In the backward pass, it uses a straight-through estimator (STE), so gradients can pass backward normally and update the floating-point weights.

The paper also designed a uniform affine quantization scheme. With a scale factor and zero-point, floating-point numbers are mapped into the integer domain, and all inference computation can be done with integer operations. This includes engineering details such as careful handling of zero-point offsets and folding batch normalization layers.

Compared with post-training quantization (PTQ), QAT gives the model a chance to adjust parameters during training and compensate for quantization error. So at the same bit-width, it usually preserves higher accuracy. This became an important technical foundation for deploying later large models on edge devices.

## Summary

The breakthroughs of 2017 formed the foundation of modern large models. Transformer gave the architectural base, RLHF pointed toward alignment, PPO provided an optimization tool, V100 supplied compute, and QAT addressed deployment efficiency.

[Image 3]

The defining feature of the year was a paradigm shift. Transformer replaced recurrent structures with attention. RLHF replaced hand-written reward functions with human preferences. V100 replaced purely general-purpose cores with specialized compute blocks. These were not incremental improvements. They changed how people thought about the problem.

It is worth noticing that their impact was not equally visible at the time. Transformer quickly triggered a revolution in NLP, while the value of RLHF became widely understood only after ChatGPT. Technology often needs time, plus the right combination with other technologies, before its value becomes obvious.

From an engineering point of view, the technologies of 2017 already revealed the core challenges of training and deploying large models: compute bottlenecks, alignment, and efficiency optimization. Much of the progress in later years was a deeper and more refined answer to exactly these problems.
